Parametric vs non-parametric statistical tests¶

Introduction¶

Before, I mentioned that a histogram of the mean of all possible means will be normally distributed. Actually things are a bit more complicated than that. No surprise! In the grander scheme of things, there are two types of statistical tests, called parametric and non-parametric tests. These two groups exist, because using the appropriate type of test for an appropriate setting allows for more accurate anlaysis and a more proper representation of what we are trying to achieve (infering statistics on population parameters).

The QQ plot¶

A parametric test is used when the sample data is taken from a normal distribution and a non-parametric test if it is not. Wow! We only have our sample data. How can we know this? Well, there's the probability plot from the *scipy.stats* library. It is more commonly known as the QQ-plot. It plots every data point against its quartile on a Cartesian plane. It then gives you a visual representation of how correlated these are. If they are not, the data is not from a normal distribution and you have to use a non-parametric test.

First, I'll generate 40 random values from the normal distribution, and constrain it to a mean of 100 and a standand deviation of 20.

In [3]:
rv1_values = stats.norm.rvs(loc = 100, scale = 20, size = 40)

Now let's do the QQ plot

The *stats.probplot()* function takes three arguments. The first is the acutal data values, the second teels the software to compare our values to normal distribution. If I wanted to see if my valuyes fit some other distribution I could have used something other than 'norm' and lastly we plot.

The plot will state a R2 value. A value of 1.0 means an absolute fit and 0.0 means no fit (i.e. realy, really not from a normal distribution.

In [4]:
plt.figure(figsize = (10,8))
stats.probplot(rv1_values, dist="norm", plot=plt) # 
plt.show();

Now I'll generate 40 values from anotehr type of continuous random distribution called the exponential distribution and see how it compares.

Now we only get an *0.9482* fit. It's really difficult to say what the exact cut-off is. Clearly an *R2* value of *0.9482* indicates that the sample was not taken from a normal distribution.
